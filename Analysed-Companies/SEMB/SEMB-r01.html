🏢 Company Identity & Background

Who is SMB Finance, and how does it make money?

SMB Finance PLC is a small, licensed non-bank finance company (NBFI) regulated by the Central Bank of Sri Lanka and listed on the CSE. It was incorporated in 1992 as Seylan Merchant Bank PLC, originally a merchant-banking subsidiary of the Seylan Bank / Ceylinco group. It later became SMB Leasing PLC (name change in March 2010) and most recently SMB Finance PLC (renamed December 2022) as it broadened from pure leasing into a fuller finance-company product suite.

Business model, explained simply: Think of SMB Finance as a small neighbourhood lender. It takes in money from depositors (fixed deposits, savings) and investors, and lends that money out as:

  • Vehicle leasing — financing for new and used cars, vans, and commercial vehicles
  • Loans — property/mortgage loans, personal loans, and gold-backed (pawning) loans
  • Treasury — placing surplus cash in government securities, REPOs, and bank placements (this is actually the company's single biggest revenue contributor, more than lending itself)
  • Money brokering — acting as a middleman in interbank call money, FX, and government securities markets

The profit comes from the spread — charging borrowers more interest than it pays depositors — plus fees from brokering and treasury gains.

Scale reality check: With total assets of only ~LKR 7 billion and 64–76 employees, SMB Finance is a micro-cap NBFI — roughly 1/15th to 1/30th the size of established peers like CDB or People's Leasing. It competes in a crowded field of 30+ licensed finance companies in Sri Lanka, most of which are dwarfed by bank-owned giants (LOLC, Commercial Bank-affiliated, People's Bank-affiliated entities).

Market position: SMB Finance is not a market leader — it is a small, niche player. Its main competitors include Citizens Development Business Finance (CDB), People's Leasing & Finance, Vallibel Finance, Senkadagala Finance, and dozens of other licensed finance companies competing for the same vehicle-leasing and gold-loan customers.

⚠️ Crisis Resilience

How has this company (and its predecessor) handled past shocks?

The 2009 Seylan Merchant Bank Crisis

This is essential history for any investor in SEMB.N0000. In 2009, when the company was still Seylan Merchant Bank PLC, it became entangled in the broader collapse of the Ceylinco Group finance arm and the Golden Key Credit Card Company failure — one of Sri Lanka's largest financial scandals of the era. Reported issues at the time included:

  • Restricted depositor withdrawals and a loss of public trust
  • Central Bank, Inland Revenue, Labour Department, and CID investigations into alleged mismanagement and financial misappropriation
  • Failure to hold Annual General Meetings for two consecutive years (2007/2008), a breach of company law
  • Large non-performing inter-company loans, including funding a loss-making affiliated automobile business
  • Failure to pay previously declared dividends due to solvency concerns

The company has since been restructured, renamed twice (to SMB Leasing PLC in 2010, then SMB Finance PLC in 2022), and rebuilt under new ownership and management — current CEO Supul Wijesinghe only joined in 2020, well after this period. There is no evidence in this research of a repeat of 2009-style misconduct under the current structure, but the history is a meaningful scar on the company's credibility that a cautious investor should weigh.

2022 Sri Lanka economic crisis: Like all Sri Lankan NBFIs, SMB Finance operated through the 2022 sovereign default, hyperinflation spike, and interest-rate shock. The company's revenue actually grew through this period (LKR 192m in 2021 to LKR 208m in 2022), suggesting it survived the immediate crisis without a collapse, though margins and provisioning likely tightened — provision for loan losses swung sharply in 2022-2023.
