🏛️ Company Background & Identity
The Origin Story

Picture Sri Lanka in 1984 — a young nation with a housing shortage and most people unable to get a simple home loan from commercial banks. To solve this, the government created the Housing Development Finance Corporation, a building society dedicated to giving ordinary Sri Lankans — especially low-to-middle income families — a way to own their homes.

Over four decades, HDFC evolved. In 2003, it became a Licensed Specialised Bank (LSB) under the CBSL, upgrading from a simple building society to a proper bank. Then in 2005, it listed on the Colombo Stock Exchange, allowing ordinary investors to own a piece of Sri Lanka's housing finance story.

Today it serves over 500,000 customers across 39 branches with 462 employees. It holds the status of one of only four Licensed Specialised Banks in Sri Lanka authorised to offer EPF (Employees' Provident Fund)-backed housing loans — a huge competitive moat.

How HDFC Makes Money (Business Model — Simply Explained)

Think of HDFC like a middleman between savers and homebuyers. Here's the simple loop:

① Sri Lankans deposit money (savings, fixed deposits) at HDFC → HDFC pays them interest (say 9-12%). ② HDFC lends that money to people who want to buy or build homes → HDFC charges them higher interest (say 14-18%). ③ The difference is HDFC's profit — called the Net Interest Margin (NIM).

The unique twist: HDFC is one of only 4 banks allowed to give loans backed by your EPF (retirement savings fund). If you're a government employee with Rs 1 Mn in your EPF, HDFC can lend you money using that EPF as security — much lower risk, because if you default, EPF pays the arrears annually.

💡 Key Resilience Insight — The "EPF Moat"

HDFC's greatest crisis protection is its EPF loan structure. When a borrower defaults on an EPF-backed loan, the Employees' Provident Fund automatically reimburses arrears annually. This means the Rs 17.75 Bn EPF loan portfolio has a built-in government backstop — the government's pension fund bears the credit risk. No private bank has this advantage. In a banking crisis, this makes HDFC significantly safer than it looks on paper.
