📖 Phase 1 — The Story So Far

How a small Nugegoda micro-lender became a CSE-listed, bank-backed finance company.

From microfinance pioneer to listed NBFI

HNB Finance began life in 2000 as Berendina Micro Investments, one of Sri Lanka's earliest commercial (i.e., profit-making, not purely charitable) microfinance lenders — built around the simple idea of giving small, fast loans to rural entrepreneurs who banks usually ignore: three-wheeler drivers, vegetable vendors, home-based tailors. In 2011, Hatton National Bank — Sri Lanka's largest private bank — bought a controlling stake, renamed the company HNB Finance, and plugged it into HNB's branch and ATM network. Chaminda Prabhath, who joined as Director of Finance that same year, became MD/CEO in 2012 and has run the company ever since.

In February 2020, HNB Finance listed on the CSE via an Offer for Sale that was oversubscribed by 32% on day one — a sign of strong investor appetite right before COVID hit. The shares formally began trading on the Diri Savi Board on 20 May 2020.

The Prime Finance merger (2021–2022)

The Central Bank of Sri Lanka has, for years, pushed a "consolidation masterplan" to shrink Sri Lanka's crowded field of small finance companies into fewer, stronger ones. In December 2021, HNB Finance bought Prime Lands' 87.27% controlling stake in Prime Finance PLC for Rs 2.1 billion, then ran a mandatory takeover offer for the remaining minority shareholders, and finally amalgamated the two companies — with HNB Finance as the surviving entity. This is why Prime Lands ended up as HNB Finance's second-largest shareholder (23.29%) for the next four years, with board seats to match.

The May 2026 ownership shake-up

In a major development just weeks before this report, Prime Lands (Pvt) Ltd fully exited HNB Finance, selling its entire 23.29% stake (400 million shares) for Rs 3.52 billion at Rs 8.80/share. Seylan Bank PLC and Phantom Investments (the investment vehicle of media entrepreneur Rayynor Silva) jointly picked up 5.82% of that block; the rest was placed with the broader market. This is Rayynor Silva's first entry into a listed finance company, on top of his recent 10% stake build-up in Seylan Bank itself.

What this means for you as a shareholder: the company's "real-estate-developer" shareholder is gone, replaced by a more bank/media-finance-oriented investor base. HNB's 51% control is untouched, so the bank's oversight and implicit support remain the anchor of the investment case — but watch for board reshuffles at the upcoming AGM (30 June 2026) given Prime Lands' board representative is likely to step down.

Business model — how HNB Finance actually makes money

Imagine a savings account and a loan book as two sides of a see-saw. HNB Finance collects fixed deposits and savings from the public (it pays interest on these — "Interest Paid on Deposits"), then lends that money out at a higher rate through four product lines: SME/Micro loans (small business loans), Leasing/Auto loans, Gold loans (pawning jewellery for quick cash — popular when households are short on cash), and Housing/personal loans. The gap between what it pays depositors and what it earns from borrowers — the Net Interest Margin — is the engine room of profit. As of FY23, vehicle financing and microfinance together made up about 65% of the loan book.

Market position

HNB Finance holds roughly a 3% share of Sri Lanka's licensed finance-company sector by assets — a small player, not a market leader. Its closest competitors are LOLC Finance, People's Leasing, Senkadagala Finance, Commercial Leasing & Finance, and Vallibel Finance. Its edge over these independent NBFIs is the HNB brand and balance-sheet backing — its biggest disadvantage is scale and a still-developing risk track record in unsecured microfinance.

🏛️ Phase 2 — Management & Governance Deep Dive

Who's actually running this company, and are there any red flags?

CEO tenure: 🟢 Good

Chaminda Prabhath has been MD/CEO since March 2012 — over 13 years in the seat. In a sector where management churn is a real risk signal, this kind of long, stable tenure through both boom (2020 IPO) and bust (2022 crisis loss) is a genuine positive. He previously worked at Prime Lands itself (2010–2011) before joining HNB Finance.

Chairman rotation: 🟢 Good (normal)

Chairmen have rotated roughly every 2-3 years — Jonathan Alles (HNB Group MD/CEO, 2014–2021) → Dilshan Rodrigo (HNB COO, 2021–2024) → Rajive Dissanayake (HNB EVP, since Jan 2024). This isn't boardroom instability; it reflects HNB's normal practice of seconding senior bank executives to chair its subsidiaries.

Board composition: 🟡 Caution

The board mixes HNB-nominated directors, independent directors (including a finance professor, ex-airline CFO, and microfinance specialists from India), and — until May 2026 — Prime Lands-linked directors (P.A.W. Perera, B. Premalal). With Prime Lands' full exit, those board seats are likely to be vacated or replaced soon; watch the AGM (30 June 2026) for confirmation.

Related-party exposure: 🟡 Caution (historical)

The 2021 Prime Finance acquisition was itself a related-party-adjacent transaction (Prime Lands sold a company to HNB Finance while also sitting on HNB Finance's board). It was conducted via a CBSL-sanctioned, regulator-approved consolidation process with an independent mandatory-offer mechanism for minorities — a legitimate, disclosed deal rather than a hidden one. No other major related-party loan flows to director-owned entities are evident in public filings.

Capital-adequacy near-miss (2023–24): 🔴 Red flag (resolved)

This is the single most important governance/risk fact in this report. Fitch Ratings disclosed that HNB Finance's Tier 1 Capital Ratio fell to 7.2% at end-3QFY24 — below the 8.5% regulatory minimum — with leverage at 8.3× (one of the highest among Fitch-rated Sri Lankan finance companies) and gross Stage 3 (non-performing) loans rising to 24% of the book, concentrated in unsecured microfinance. This was a genuine near-breach, not a rumour.

Capital raise & rating recovery: 🟢 Good (turnaround executed)

Management responded with equity infusions (a rights issue raising ~Rs 1.19bn in FY25, plus further capital actions in FY26 that grew shares outstanding ~17% YoY). By FY24/25 year-end, the company reported its Tier 1 and Total Risk-Weighted Capital Ratios had improved to levels "well above regulatory thresholds," and Fitch upgraded HNBF's National Long-Term Rating to A(lka) Stable from BBB+(lka) during the year. This is a credible, verifiable turnaround — but it also explains the dilution shareholders absorbed, and asset quality (NPL ratio) in the microfinance book deserves continued monitoring in each new quarterly release.

ESG / sustainability signalling: 🟢 Good

HNB Finance has repeatedly been certified a "Great Place to Work" (4+ consecutive years) and runs CSR programmes (COVID relief funds, school infrastructure, disaster relief). No major ESG controversies were found in public records.

Beginner note on "Debt-to-Equity" for a lender: for a normal company, D/E compares borrowed money to owners' money. For a deposit-taking lender like HNB Finance, the real leverage also includes customer deposits (Rs 56.7bn at TTM) — deposits aren't "debt" in the conventional sense, but they are still other people's money funding the loan book. Including deposits, HNBF's true leverage is around 8-9× equity — typical, even necessary, for a finance company's business model, but it does mean a relatively small percentage loss on the loan book can hurt equity disproportionately. This is exactly what happened in FY2023.

3C — Cash Flow Statement

Operating Cash Flow vs. Net Profit, FY2022–FY2026 (Rs millions).

Why Operating Cash Flow looks so negative — and why that's not necessarily alarming here: Under the accounting template used for lenders, money lent out to new customers counts as an "operating" cash outflow, while the deposits that fund that lending count as a "financing" inflow. HNB Finance's loan book grew from Rs 45.4bn to Rs 62.6bn in roughly 18 months — that growth alone explains almost all of the Rs 29.1bn "negative operating cash flow" in FY2026. This is structurally different from a normal company burning cash it can't replace. The real question for a growing lender is not "is OCF negative?" but "is the loan growth funded sustainably (deposits/equity) and is asset quality holding up?" — which is exactly what Phase 3E investigates below.

Score: 5 out of 9. A "textbook" Piotroski score below 7 would normally flag a deteriorating company — but three of the four failed criteria here (CFO, leverage, dilution) are direct, explainable consequences of a deliberate, regulator-encouraged capital raise and fast loan-book growth at a recovering finance company, not signs of a business quietly rotting from within. The two genuine "pass with caution" areas to track every quarter going forward are leverage and asset quality (NPL ratio).

Why Altman Z-Score is skipped here: the classic Altman Z-Score formula assumes a normal operating company where high leverage signals distress. Deposit-taking lenders are supposed to be highly leveraged (that's literally the business model — borrow cheap via deposits, lend dearer). Applying the textbook formula to any bank or finance company produces a misleadingly low, scary-looking number that professional analysts simply don't use for this sector. The better solvency signals for an NBFI are: (1) regulatory capital ratios — HNBF's are now "well above" the CBSL minimum after the FY25 capital raise, and (2) the independent credit rating trend — Fitch's upgrade to A(lka) Stable in FY24/25 (up from BBB+(lka)) is the market's own version of an Altman Z-Score for this sector, and it's moving in the right direction.
