Phase 0 – 1 · Company Identity & Background

The Story: From One Spray Gun to the CSE

In 1998, a young entrepreneur named Denham Marlon Ferreira started a pest-control business in Colombo with an initial investment of just LKR 75,000 (about USD 1,000 at the time) — one person, one spray gun, and a motorbike. That one-man operation is today Exterminators PLC, a company employing roughly 33 people across five branches (Colombo, Kandy, Galle, Malabe, and Dambulla), and — since 30 March 2022 — the first pest-management company in Asia to list on a public stock exchange.

1998 — Marlon Ferreira founds the business as a sole proprietorship.
2004 — Formally incorporated in Sri Lanka as a private limited liability company.
2009 — Invents the "Smart Missile" termite-bait station, South Asia's first locally manufactured device of its kind (carbon-neutral certified in 2015; relaunched as an AI-powered "3G" version in 2026).
2014 — Launches South Asia's first mobile app for the pest-management industry, starting a decade-long digitalisation push (e-Scheduler in 2018, the "eConnect" customer portal in 2019).
2020 — COVID-19 hits. Exterminators converts its chemical-spraying fleet to disinfection services, becomes the first in the region with COVID-19 SLS hygiene certification, and disinfects 1.2M+ sq. ft. of public space (including 77 elders'/children's homes) free of charge — a genuine crisis pivot that also generated new revenue.
Nov 2021 — Converts to a Public Limited Company ahead of listing.
14–30 Mar 2022 — IPO on the CSE's Empower Board (the board for small & medium enterprises): 10,000,000 new shares at LKR 6.00, raising LKR 60 million for a 20% stake. Manager to the issue: Atarah Capital Partners.
2022–23 — Sri Lanka's economic crisis hits import costs hard. Management responds with "Made in Sri Lanka" — shifting from imported chemical formulations to local synthesis — plus AI-driven route optimisation to cut fuel costs, and pays down nearly all bank debt using IPO proceeds.
Feb 2024 — Veteran corporate leader Dr. Kishu Gomes (former 18-year MD/CEO of Chevron Lubricants Lanka, former Chairman of Sri Lanka Tourism) becomes Independent Non-Executive Chairman, succeeding founding Chairman Chaaminda Ratnayake.
Jul 2024 — Acquires Exterminator International (Private) Limited to pursue franchising/licensing in South Asia and beyond.
2025–26 — Deploys AI-powered pest-prediction and route-optimisation tools; exports proprietary systems like the "Smart Missile" to Seychelles and "Smart Guard" to Velana International Airport (Maldives); reports a 275% net-profit rebound in FY2025/26.

How does the company actually make money?

Think of Exterminators as a subscription business wearing overalls. Most revenue comes from recurring service contracts — monthly or quarterly visits to spray, inspect, and maintain bait stations at hotels, factories, food plants, condominiums, schools, and even aircraft/ships (much like a gym membership, but for keeping pests out). On top of that, it sells pest-control hardware (electric fly killers, rodent stations, termite systems) and, since 2020, disinfection & sanitation services. The newest growth lever is franchising/licensing its brand and technology abroad (Maldives, Seychelles, with ambitions in the UAE and Botswana) — a low-capital way to earn royalty-style income outside Sri Lanka.

Ownership & market position

Founder Marlon Ferreira remains the controlling shareholder (reportedly ~68.6% of shares — see governance caveats below), so this is very much a founder-led company. Exterminators describes itself as Sri Lanka's largest formal/branded pest-management operator and the only one listed on any stock exchange; the rest of the local market is fragmented among smaller, unlisted operators, so direct apples-to-apples local competitor data is not readily available. Internationally, Exterminators is a small minnow next to global giants like Rentokil Initial and Rollins (which together dominate a global pest-control market now worth roughly USD 26–28 billion and growing 5–8% a year) — but its tiny size also means even modest regional wins (e.g., a Maldives or Seychelles franchise) can meaningfully move its numbers.

Phase 2 · Management & Governance Deep Dive

Who's Running the Company — and How Well?

Governance is basically asking: "Do the people in charge treat shareholders' money like it's their own — or like it's someone else's?" Here's an honest, item-by-item look, rated 🟢 Good / 🟡 Caution / 🔴 Red Flag.

🟢
Independent Chairman upgrade. Dr. Kishu Gomes — an 18-year multinational CEO (Chevron Lubricants Lanka) and former Chairman of Sri Lanka Tourism — took over as Independent Non-Executive Chairman in Feb 2024, replacing the founding chairman. This is a genuine credibility upgrade for a small-cap.
🟢
Board diversification. The board includes operational, family, and independent directors, including Jacob Laubscher (international pest-tech/digitalisation expert) appointed Jan 2024 — adding real technical oversight, not just rubber-stamping.
🟡
Founder concentration. Marlon Ferreira is reported to hold roughly 68.6% of shares. High founder ownership can align incentives well, but it also means minority shareholders have very little say, and free-float liquidity is naturally thin.
🟡
Thin public float. Secondary-source data (not independently confirmed by us against a primary CSE disclosure) suggests the public float may be around 17–18% of shares — below the CSE's continuing-listing minimum of 20%. If accurate, this is worth the investor confirming directly via CSE/company disclosures, as low float drives the stock's outsized weekly price swings (≈9–10% vs a CSE average under 4.5%).
🟡
Reported insider selling. Some secondary sources report the Managing Director reduced his personal holdings meaningfully over the past year, including a multi-million-share on-market sale in early 2026. We could not independently verify the exact figures via a primary CSE filing in the time available — treat this as a flag to verify yourself before acting on it, not a confirmed fact.
🟡
Reported audit-qualification flag (2024). Some research sources reference a CSE notice that the company's FY ended March 2024 financial statements drew a qualified audit opinion, triggering a "Watch List" disclosure process under CSE rules. We were not able to independently verify this against the primary CSE announcement within this research session — please verify directly on the CSE website before treating it as confirmed. We did independently confirm that FY2023/24 reporting includes unusual one-off items (a large loss on sale of assets) that would plausibly attract auditor scrutiny.
What this means in plain English: the leadership upgrade (Dr. Gomes, an outside tech-savvy director) is a real positive — it suggests the board is trying to professionalise. But this remains a tightly-controlled, thinly-traded micro-cap where a handful of unverified-but-plausible flags (low float, reported insider sales, a reported audit qualification) mean you should do your own confirmation via CSE filings before investing meaningfully, and size any position accordingly.

The single most important finding in this report: FY26's headline "275% profit rebound" looks fantastic on the income statement, but the cash flow statement tells a more cautious story — operating cash flow badly lagged profit, and customer receivables ballooned nearly 10x faster than revenue. This doesn't mean the turnaround is fake, but it does mean it isn't fully proven in cash yet. A genuinely durable recovery should show next year's operating cash flow catching up to net profit, and receivable growth slowing back toward revenue growth.

Phase 6 · Global Sector Context & Macro

What's Happening in the World That Matters Here

Globally, the pest-control industry is a steady, defensive growth business — worth roughly USD 26–28 billion in 2026 and growing 5–8% a year, driven by urbanisation, climate-driven pest proliferation, rising hygiene awareness, and a wave of consolidation (Rentokil's 2025 acquisition of Terminix being the largest recent example) plus AI-powered pest-prediction tools — exactly the trend Exterminators is trying to ride locally with its own AI push. The structural tailwind is real, but Exterminators is a tiny, single-country player competing for relevance against giants with vastly more capital.
